# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

**International visitors** (donors, community walk tourists, journalists, NGO partners): arriving with curiosity or intention to give, they need trust signals fast — specific numbers, real programs, authentic imagery — and a frictionless path to donate or book a walk.

**Local community members** (program participants, youth families, women in skills training, elderly residents): arriving to find what's available to them, they need program clarity, contact routes, and a tone that speaks to them as neighbors, not beneficiaries.

Both audiences arrive at the same front door. The site must serve both without compromising either.

## Product Purpose

Engolon CBO's website is the organization's primary face to the world. It communicates who the organization is and what it does, earns the trust and financial support of international donors and tourists, and provides community members with clear access to programs and resources.

Success looks like: sustained donations, consistent walk bookings, active volunteer interest, and community members who recognize themselves in the site.

## Brand Personality

Authentic · Resilient · Hopeful

The organization comes from the community it serves — Kibra and Kajiado, specifically. The voice is grounded and direct, celebrating what exists rather than mourning what's lacking. It speaks with the confidence of people who have been doing this work without waiting for permission.

Emotional goal: visitors should feel moved by the specificity of the work, not manipulated by its difficulty. Pride, not pity.

## Anti-references

- **Generic charity blue**: the clean-blue, stock-photo, "helping hands" aesthetic of large international aid organizations (UNICEF, Red Cross visual register). Engolon is local-first; it should feel situated in Nairobi, not headquartered in Geneva.
- **Poverty porn**: sad close-up imagery, guilt-tripping copy, framing the community as helpless or pitiable. The site should read as celebration and capability.
- **Corporate nonprofit**: polished but soulless — the Fortune 500 CSR report aesthetic. Impeccable brand consistency with no actual warmth or humanity.

## Design Principles

1. **Show, don't generalize.** Real names, real locations (Kibra, Kajiado), real pricing, real program durations. Specificity builds trust; abstraction erodes it.
2. **Practice what we preach.** The organization runs a PWD inclusion program. The site itself must be fully accessible — WCAG AAA is not aspirational here, it is the standard.
3. **Dignity in every frame.** Every image choice and copy line either affirms the agency and humanity of community members, or it doesn't appear.
4. **Both audiences, one voice.** The donor and the community member read the same sentences. Write for both: clear enough for someone who doesn't know Kibra, warm enough for someone who grew up there.
5. **Local color, not local cliché.** The dark editorial aesthetic and warm orange palette are deliberate — rooted in the place without trading in stereotypes.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AAA target. Given the organization's PWD inclusion and digital access programs, full accessibility is a brand value, not just a compliance check. Covers: color contrast (≥7:1 for normal text), keyboard navigation, screen reader support, reduced motion alternatives, semantic structure, and focus management for all interactive elements.

Language: English only for current version. Swahili support is a noted future direction.
