Big idea

Module 2 · Introduction to Drone Systems and Control Design

A drone is an aerial robotic system. Stable and useful behavior depends on propulsion, sensors, a flight controller, a communication link, and a mission-specific payload working together under continuous feedback control.

Drone subsystems

1
Frame Physical structure that holds all components and absorbs impact.
2
Propulsion Motors and propellers that generate lift and directional movement.
3
Power Battery and power distribution board supplying all onboard systems.
4
Sensors Estimate altitude, orientation, position, speed, and environment.
5
Flight controller Processes sensor data and sends commands to the motors continuously.
6
Communication link Connects the drone to the operator, base station, or network.
7
Payload Mission equipment: camera, sensor array, delivery package, or inspection tool.

The autonomy stack

1
Perception Detect relevant information from the environment using sensors.
2
Localization Estimate where the drone is — position, altitude, and orientation.
3
Planning Choose a path or action based on the mission goal.
4
Control Convert the plan into motor commands using feedback control.
5
Mission monitoring Check safety, progress, and constraints throughout the flight.
Mission monitoring feeds back into Perception — the loop runs continuously during flight.

Core terms

Roll

Tilting the drone left or right. Controlled by varying the speed of left versus right motors. Used to move sideways or bank into a turn.

Pitch

Tilting the drone forward or backward. Used to accelerate or decelerate horizontally — tilting forward drives the drone ahead.

Yaw

Rotating around the drone's vertical axis — like spinning a top without tilting. Achieved by varying the spin direction of opposing motor pairs.

Altitude

Height above a reference point such as sea level, ground level, or the takeoff point. Controlled by adjusting all four motor speeds together.

Flight controller

The drone's onboard computer. Reads sensors hundreds of times per second and adjusts individual motor speeds to maintain stable, commanded flight.

Error (control)

The difference between what the system is asked to do (the command) and what it is actually doing (the response). The controller works to reduce this gap.

PID controller

Proportional-Integral-Derivative control. P responds to the current error. I corrects for accumulated error over time. D dampens the rate of change to prevent overshoot.

Stability

Whether the drone returns to its intended state after a disturbance — such as a wind gust or an unexpected payload shift.

Overshoot

When the drone goes past its target — altitude, position, or heading — before the controller pulls it back. A sign that controller gains need tuning.

Payload

The mission equipment the drone carries. The payload's weight, center of mass, and power draw directly affect how the flight controller must be tuned.
